How I Built
TryCareerMatch

From a personal frustration to a live product — the decisions, trade-offs, and lessons from building TryCareerMatch end-to-end.

Product Management UX Design Scoring Model Web App
9 min read

TryCareerMatch started as a personal problem. After years of working across design, digital marketing, and financial analysis, I found myself struggling to answer one deceptively simple question: which product roles actually fit my background? Job descriptions were written for archetypal candidates, not for people whose skills had been built across multiple fields. Standard career tools gave me personality types and generic advice. None of them looked at what I could actually do and mapped it to what specific roles required.

So I built it myself. What follows is the honest account of how that happened — the problem definition, the product decisions, the trade-offs I made, and what I'd do differently.

Discovery

The problem: skills without a map

The core frustration was this: most career discovery tools either tell you what you are (a personality type, a strengths profile) or show you what's available (job listings). Neither answers the question that actually matters for a career transition: given what I can do, which roles am I closest to — and which skills are holding me back?

The people who feel this most acutely are professionals with non-linear or cross-functional backgrounds. Specialists fit neatly into their category. Generalists, career changers, and people who've built skills across multiple domains are left to piece together their own map.

What existing tools offer
Personality-type quizzes (MBTI, CliftonStrengths)
Generic role suggestions ("You might like Marketing")
Job boards filtered by title or keyword
Salary benchmarks by role and location

Useful — but none of them take your actual skills as input.
What TryCareerMatch does instead
Takes your skill levels across 88 competencies
Maps them against 60+ specific roles
Shows match % for each role based on weighted scoring
Identifies which skills would most improve a specific match

No personality test. No registration. Just skills in, match out.
The framing that unlocked the product: stop thinking about "what type of person are you?" and start asking "which roles value the skills you've already built?" These are very different questions with very different answers.
Product Concept

The core decision: mapping, not matching

Early in the design process, I had two competing models in mind.

The first was a quiz approach: ask users a series of questions about how they like to work, what energizes them, and how they handle conflict. This is what most career tools do. It's engaging, but it produces personality profiles — not skill-to-role mappings. And it's inherently subjective; people answer based on self-perception, not capability.

The second was a skills inventory approach: ask users to rate their proficiency across a defined set of skills, then score those against a model of what each role requires. Less fun, more useful. That's what I built.

88 Skills
Enough to cover professional competencies meaningfully — not so many that users quit halfway through. Organized into 9 categories (Data, Finance, Product, Design, Management, etc.).
60+ Roles
Specific enough to be useful — "Product Manager" and "Product Owner" are separate entries with distinct weight profiles. Covers 9 sectors from Tech to Finance to Government.
Zero Registration
No account, no email, no friction. The decision to skip registration came from one principle: the product only delivers value in the moment of use, so nothing should stand between the user and their result.
Data Model

Designing the scoring system

The hardest part of TryCareerMatch wasn't the code. It was building the weight matrix — assigning a relevance score (0 to 3) to every skill-role combination. That's roughly 5,280 individual data points, each requiring a judgment call about how much a given skill matters for a given role.

Weight 0
Not relevant. Having this skill doesn't improve the match score for this role. Example: 3D Visualisation for a Financial Analyst role.
Weight 1
Helpful. The skill is a minor positive signal — useful context but not expected. Example: basic SQL for a Product Manager role.
Weight 2
Important. The role benefits significantly from this skill. Most hiring managers would expect it. Example: Excel Advanced for a Financial Analyst.
Weight 3
Core requirement. This is what the role is fundamentally about. Absence would be a significant red flag. Example: Variance Analysis for a Financial Controller.

The scoring formula is straightforward: for each role, sum the products of (user skill level × role weight) across all 88 skills, then normalize to a percentage. A score of 85% means you have 85% of the weighted skill value that the ideal candidate for that role would have.

The most important calibration decision was role differentiation. If PM and PO had nearly identical weight profiles, the tool would produce nearly identical scores for both — useless. I had to make the distinctions sharp enough to be meaningful while still being accurate to what those roles actually require.

The weight matrix is the core IP of the product. Getting it right required research into job descriptions, conversations with professionals in those roles, and multiple rounds of calibration. It's also where AI was most useful — not to replace judgment, but to accelerate the research phase significantly.
UX & Interface

The interface decisions that shaped the experience

With 88 skills to rate, the UX challenge was clear: how do you get users to complete a 88-item assessment without abandoning it halfway through? The answer was a combination of structure, progressive disclosure, and visual clarity.

01
Group skills into categories
Presenting 88 items as a flat list would be overwhelming. Grouping them into 9 categories (Data, Finance, Product, Design, etc.) makes the task feel manageable and lets users scan for their domain quickly.
02
4-level proficiency scale
None / Basic / Intermediate / Advanced. Four levels are enough to differentiate meaningful signal. More levels create decision fatigue; fewer levels lose precision. I tested both and this range struck the right balance.
03
Results sorted by match score
Show the top 10 roles by match percentage. Showing all 60+ at once would be noisy; showing only 3-5 would miss legitimate close-second options. Top 10 covers the decision space without overwhelming.
04
Sector color-coding
Color-coding results by sector (Tech, Finance, Healthcare, etc.) lets users quickly see if they're clustering in one area or have cross-sector fit. Pattern recognition at a glance — no table reading required.

The biggest UX debate I had with myself was whether to show skill gap analysis — which specific skills, if improved, would most move the score for a target role. I ultimately built a simplified version of this, though it didn't make the initial launch. It's on the backlog.

Build & Launch

Stack, timeline, and what AI changed

The technical stack was deliberately minimal: HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript. No framework. The entire data model — all 5,280 skill-role weights — lives in a single JavaScript object. The scoring engine is about 40 lines of code. Everything else is interface.

I chose this stack for two reasons. First, simplicity: fewer moving parts means fewer things to debug, and a static site deploys in seconds. Second, performance: no server-side processing, no API calls on the critical path — results are calculated client-side instantly.

Build time: ~3 weeks
Week 1: problem definition and data model design. Week 2: weight matrix research and calibration. Week 3: UI build and testing. The data work took as long as the code work.
AI as a research accelerator
AI didn't write the product — it accelerated the research phase. Calibrating weights for 60+ roles would have taken months of manual job description analysis. With AI-assisted research, it took days.

Launch was quiet by design. I shared it in a few communities where the problem resonated — professionals in career transition, people with non-linear backgrounds — and let organic sharing do the rest. The no-registration model made sharing frictionless: send someone a link, they use it immediately, no account required.

Retrospective

What worked, what I'd change

TryCareerMatch has had real users and real sessions. More importantly, it solved the problem I originally had — and I've heard from others who found it genuinely useful for their own career thinking. That's the metric that matters most at this stage.

But honest retrospectives require acknowledging what didn't work or what I'd do differently. Three things stand out:

What worked: zero friction
The no-registration decision was correct. Completion rates are meaningfully higher than I'd expected for an 88-item assessment. When there's no account to create, users commit to the task instead of deferring it.
What worked: role specificity
Keeping roles specific (not collapsing "Product Manager" and "Product Owner" into one) generated much richer results for users with nuanced backgrounds. The granularity was worth the extra calibration effort.
What worked: fast results
Client-side scoring means results appear instantly. No loading screen, no waiting. In a tool where the payoff comes at the end of an 88-item assessment, instant results feel like a reward — not a feature.
What I'd change: result persistence
Many users wanted to save their results or revisit them later. Without an account, results disappear when the session ends. A lightweight save-via-URL mechanism (encoding results in the URL hash) would solve this without requiring registration.
What I'd change: skill gap view
The most requested feature: "for this role, which skills am I missing most?" A skill gap breakdown per role would turn TryCareerMatch from a discovery tool into an active development tool — a much stronger value proposition.
What I'd change: mobile UX
Rating 88 skills on a small screen is functional but not elegant. A tabbed mobile view — one category at a time with a progress indicator — would significantly improve the mobile experience, which is where a surprising share of sessions come from.
The most useful product retrospective question: "If I started over today with everything I now know, what would I do differently in week one?" For TryCareerMatch, the answer is: start with the mobile experience, not the desktop one. Mobile was an afterthought in the build — it shouldn't have been.
